A Visual History of Climate Change

An overview of how we're warming the planet, what that means for us, and what we can do about it.

By Nehal Udyavar

The Beginning

Roughly two hundred thousand years ago, a new species emerged in a small slice of eastern Africa. The species did not look particularly distinctive – in fact, there were similar looking species roaming the cold plains of Europe and the tropics of eastern Asia for millions of years. They were not particularly strong or quick; like most other animals, they spent most of their time gathering plants and hunting animals for food, protecting their territories from predators, and occasionally, reproducing.

Evolution, however, would decide to be kind to this particular species, gradually developing the brain of the Homo sapiens. While the brain would initially be quite burdensome for the Homo genus – (the brain is a voracious consumer of energy) – it would eventually be primarily responsible for planes and agriculture and nuclear weapons.

Playing with Fire

It is difficult to identify exactly when we began our rise from the middle of the food chain to the top, but a good contender for that starting line is the domestication of fire. In fact, Neanderthals and human species pre-dating Homo sapiens used fire on a daily basis about 300,000 years ago. Fire gave humans light in the darkness, warmth in the cold. Fire enabled humans to fend off prowling predators, and change the chemistry of their food. But most notably, domesticating fire demonstrated humans’ ability to control natural forces, and manipulate a powerful form of energy to their benefit. The development of this wizardly ability would rapidly elevate Homo sapiens to the top of the food chain, and eventually rewrite the time-honored contract between Earth and its inhabitants.

Hungry for Energy

In chemistry, fire is the result of a process called combustion, which is when fuel and oxygen react to release heat and light energy. The concept of energy is very important; it is the property that allows any action to take place (or in physics terms, “do work”). Additionally, energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another. When we burn wood for our campfire, the chemical energy in the wood is being converted into heat and light energy from the flame.

Humans, however, are not unique in their ability to harness energy from plentiful sources. For example, plants use energy from the Sun to create food through a process called photosynthesis, and eagles use thermal and kinetic energy from the wind in a process called flying. Our eminence comes from that the fact that, while every other organism is limited by their energy cycles or their design, we gradually learned to expand our energy palette and sculpt it to our liking. While plants and eagles have hardly changed the way they wield energy from the Sun and wind, we have created our own mini-suns and mini-wind-systems in the form of heaters and fans.

This capability would quickly escalate to dizzying heights. From utilizing oxen muscle to increase agricultural yield and harnessing the wind to sail ships, to burning coal to power steam engines and building solar panels to power satellites; humans’ ingenuity in manipulating energy has closely tailed their demand for it.
